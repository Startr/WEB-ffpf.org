## Speaker Notes for Vinod

### SLIDE 1 — The Voice That Stays With You (60 seconds)
Open cold. No introduction, no "good evening," no pleasantries. Start with the patient. If video is available, play it — 30 to 45 seconds max, then one line from you: *"That is why we are here tonight."* If no video, retell his story in your own words. Keep it under 90 seconds. Use his name. Describe his face before surgery. Then describe what he said — not what the doctors did, what *he* said. The room needs to hear a patient's voice before they hear yours.

### SLIDE 2 — You Did That (90 seconds)
Pivot immediately to the donors. Do not linger on the Foundation or the medical team yet — that comes later. The first thing the audience hears after the patient's story is: *you did that.* This is the strategic core of the evening. Major donors with personal relationships to the organization need to feel ownership, not just appreciation. Make eye contact with specific people in the room if comfortable.

### SLIDE 3 — The Numbers From Jamkhed (90 seconds)
Deliver the numbers clean and fast. Do not editorialize each one — let the scale speak. Pause after the total. One sentence to land it: *"That was one week."* These donors are business people. They respect ROI. Show them theirs.

### SLIDE 4 — Since 1988 (90 seconds)
Widen the lens. This is where the founder story lives — but keep it brief. The point is not history. The point is: *this is not new, this is not fragile, this is a machine that works.* Major donors trust systems. Show them the system. The Dwivedi legacy line connects past to present in one beat. Do not over-narrate it — the dynasty speaks for itself.

### SLIDE 5 — Meet Archana (2.5 minutes)
Introduce Archana in two sentences. Do not over-explain. Then step aside and play the preview. Let the documentary do the work. When it ends, say nothing for three seconds. Let the room sit in it. Then: *"In November, you'll see the rest of her story."* That's it. Move on.

### SLIDE 6 — The Team Who Went (2 minutes)
This is the recognition beat. Name the team collectively first — the roles, the sacrifice, the conditions. Then ask them to stand. Hold for applause. If specific volunteers have agreed to be called out, name them here with one line each — role and one detail. Do not rush this. Volunteer retention is built in moments like this. These people need to feel seen.

### SLIDE 7 — The Question You Earned The Right To Ask (30 seconds)
This is the pivot slide. The hinge between gratitude and ask. It should feel like a natural turn, not a gear shift. You are not changing the subject — you are following the emotional logic to its conclusion. Deliver it like a boardroom insight, not a plea.

### SLIDE 8 — What Happens Next (2 minutes)
The ask. $12,000 per camp. Say the number once, clearly, without apology. Then explain what it buys — not in abstract terms, but in concrete ones. *"Surgical supplies. Patient transport. Post-operative care. A full week."* Frame it as a sponsorship, not a donation. These donors understand the difference. A donation disappears into a budget. A sponsorship has their name on it. Pause after the framing. Let people do the math in their heads.

### SLIDE 9 — November Bridge (60 seconds)
This is not a hard sell for November. It is an invitation that feels inevitable. You've shown them two minutes of Archana. They want the rest. Say so: *"You saw two minutes. There are forty more. I want you in the room when we show them."* This creates two commitments in one evening — the sponsorship and the return visit.

### SLIDE 10 — Thank You & Close (90 seconds)
Three thank-yous in sequence. Donors first — echo the opening patient, close the loop. Volunteers second — brief, warm, proud. Founder last — one line. End on *"Together, we break the poverty cycle. One camp at a time."* Hold. Do not say "thank you for coming." Do not say "enjoy your evening." The tagline is the last thing they hear. Let it land. Hold for applause.

### Total Runtime: ~15 minutes

### Critical Reminders
- **Do NOT mention dentistry camp issues.** Celebrate the medical work holistically without singling out any camp's challenges.
- **Steer clear of politics, religion, and any sensitive organizational history.** Keep the focus on patients, impact, and the future.
- **The Archana documentary preview must be cued and tested before the dinner.** Dead air while finding a video will kill the room's momentum.
- **Have camp sponsorship information (one-pagers or cards) on each table** so donors can act immediately after the presentation. Do not make them hunt for how to give.
